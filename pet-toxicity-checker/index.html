❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What should I do if my pet eats something toxic?
Stay calm and act quickly. Do not induce vomiting unless a veterinarian specifically tells you to — some substances cause more damage coming up. Remove your pet from the area. Call your veterinarian, an emergency vet clinic, or one of these 24/7 poison control hotlines: ASPCA Poison Control (1-888-426-4435) or Pet Poison Helpline (1-855-764-7661). There is a $85–95 consultation fee. Having pet insurance can help cover these costs. Save any packaging or plant material for identification.
Can dogs eat grapes?
No. Grapes and raisins are highly toxic to dogs. They can cause acute kidney failure even in small amounts. Some dogs are more sensitive than others, but there is no known safe amount. Symptoms include vomiting, lethargy, loss of appetite, and decreased urination within 12–24 hours. If your dog eats grapes or raisins, contact your vet or a poison control hotline immediately.
Why is xylitol dangerous for dogs?
Xylitol is an artificial sweetener found in sugar-free gum, candy, baked goods, peanut butter, and toothpaste. In dogs, it causes a rapid, massive insulin release leading to dangerously low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) within 10–60 minutes. Symptoms include weakness, staggering, collapse, and seizures. In high doses, xylitol can cause liver failure. Always check ingredient labels — especially on peanut butter before sharing with your dog.
Is chocolate really that bad for dogs?
Yes. Chocolate contains theobromine and caffeine, which dogs and cats cannot metabolize effectively. Dark chocolate, baking chocolate, and cocoa powder are the most dangerous (10–20x more theobromine than milk chocolate). Symptoms include vomiting, diarrhea, rapid breathing, increased heart rate, tremors, and seizures. The toxic dose depends on the type of chocolate and your dog's weight. Always consult a vet if your dog eats any amount of dark or baking chocolate.
What foods are safe for dogs to eat?
Many human foods are safe for dogs in moderation. Safe options include: cooked chicken (plain, boneless), carrots, apples (no seeds), blueberries, bananas, green beans, sweet potatoes (cooked), plain rice, oatmeal, peanut butter (xylitol-free), plain yogurt, and pumpkin. Always introduce new foods gradually and in small amounts. When in doubt, check our toxicity checker above or consult your veterinarian.
