❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What is dog separation anxiety?
Separation anxiety is a behavioral condition where a dog experiences extreme distress when separated from their owner or left alone. Unlike simple boredom or mischief, it's a genuine panic response. Symptoms include destructive behavior, excessive vocalization, potty accidents, pacing, drooling, and escape attempts. It affects an estimated 20–40% of dogs referred to veterinary behaviorists.
How is separation anxiety different from normal bad behavior?
The key difference is timing and intensity. A dog with separation anxiety only shows these behaviors when left alone or when the owner prepares to leave. They often start within minutes of departure. A bored dog might chew a shoe occasionally, but an anxious dog will frantically scratch at doors, drool excessively, or have accidents despite being house-trained — even if only left for a short time.
Can separation anxiety be cured?
Yes, most dogs improve significantly with the right approach. Treatment typically combines counter-conditioning (creating positive associations with alone time), gradual desensitization (short practice departures), environmental changes (puzzles, music), and in some cases, veterinary behaviorist guidance or medication. Our assessment provides tailored recommendations based on your dog's specific symptoms and severity level.
How long does it take to treat separation anxiety?
It varies widely depending on severity and consistency. Mild cases may show improvement in 2–4 weeks with consistent training. Moderate cases typically take 2–4 months. Severe cases may require 6+ months and often benefit from professional help from a certified behaviorist. The key is patience and consistency — never punish your dog for anxiety-related behavior, as it makes the problem worse.
Should I get another dog to keep mine company?
Getting another dog is rarely a solution for separation anxiety. Your dog's anxiety is about you leaving, not about being alone in general. Many dogs with separation anxiety still panic even when another dog is present. In fact, adding another dog can sometimes increase stress. Focus on training and behavior modification first before considering a second pet.
When should I see a veterinarian about separation anxiety?
Consult your vet if your dog's anxiety is severe — if they're injuring themselves, destroying barriers, or the behavior isn't improving with training. Your vet can rule out medical causes and may recommend a veterinary behaviorist. In some cases, anti-anxiety medication can help reduce your dog's stress enough for training to be effective.
