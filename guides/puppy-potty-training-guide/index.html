🐶 Potty Training: Step-by-Step Schedule & Crate Training That Works

Last updated: June 2026 · Based on AKC house training guidelines and veterinary behavior recommendations

Bringing home a new puppy is exciting. Cleaning up your fourth accident on the living room rug by 9 AM — less so. Here's the truth most potty training guides won't tell you: your puppy isn't being stubborn or spiteful. They literally cannot control their bladder yet. The muscles and neurological pathways that allow a dog to "hold it" don't fully develop until 4–6 months of age.

Potty training isn't about teaching your puppy to hold it — it's about you learning to predict when they need to go, managing their environment so they succeed, and celebrating every win. Get the system right, and most puppies are well on their way by 4–5 months. This guide gives you the exact system: what supplies you need, an age-by-age schedule, how to use a crate effectively, apartment strategies that work, and exactly what to do when (not if) accidents happen.

Before You Start — What You'll Need

You can't potty train effectively with just willpower. These five supplies make the difference between a smooth process and a frustrating one. Invest before the puppy arrives.

1. A crate with a divider. This is your single most important tool. A puppy's natural instinct is not to soil their sleeping space — but only if the crate is the right size. Too big, and they'll simply use one corner as a bathroom and sleep in the other. The divider lets you shrink the space as your puppy grows. A wire crate with a divider panel is the most practical choice — good airflow, easy to clean, and foldable for travel.

The Golden Rule — Proactive Scheduling

This is the single most important concept in potty training, and most new owners get it backwards. Here it is:

You set the schedule. Your puppy doesn't tell you when they need to go.

Most owners wait for their puppy to whine or scratch at the door. The problem is, by the time a puppy signals, they're already about to burst — and puppies this young have zero warning time. You need to be the one watching the clock and taking them out on your schedule, before they need to go.

Set a timer. Every time it goes off, take your puppy to their designated potty spot. No waiting for signals. No "let's see if they need to go." Just go.

Critical potty trigger times:

  • Immediately after waking up (from any nap, any time of day)
  • Within 15 minutes after eating
  • Immediately after a play session
  • Before bedtime
  • Every 60–90 minutes for puppies under 12 weeks

Step-by-Step Daily Potty Routine

Here's exactly what a successful potty training day looks like. Follow this pattern consistently and your puppy will learn the routine within days.

Step 1: Wake up → outside immediately. The moment your puppy opens their eyes, carry them outside. No detours. No playing. Straight to the potty spot. Give your cue word ("go potty" or whatever you choose), wait, and the second they go, treat and praise like they just won an Olympic medal.

Step 2: Eat → wait 15 minutes → outside. Food stimulates the digestive system. After your puppy finishes eating, set a 15-minute timer, then take them out. This is the most predictable potty trigger you'll get all day.

Step 3: Play → outside immediately. Exercise and excitement stimulate the bladder. After any play session — even a short one — take your puppy out.

Step 4: Nap → outside immediately. Waking up is the #1 potty trigger. Every single time your puppy wakes up from a nap, go outside.

Step 5: Before bed → one last trip. Take your puppy out right before you crate them for the night. Then restrict water access 2 hours before bedtime to reduce overnight accidents.

Repeat this cycle all day. It's exhausting for the first few weeks — but it builds the neural pathways your puppy needs to develop bladder control. Every success reinforces the pattern.

Crate Training for Potty Training

Here's the crate secret: dogs are den animals. They instinctively avoid soiling their sleeping area. When used correctly, a crate harnesses this instinct to teach bladder control.

Size is everything. The most common mistake is a crate that's too big. Your puppy should have just enough room to stand up, turn around, and lie down. If the crate is larger, use the divider panel to block off the extra space. As your puppy grows, move the divider back. A puppy who can sleep in one end and pee in the other has learned nothing.

Crate training progression:

  • Week 1: Feed your puppy in the crate with the door open. Build positive association.
  • Week 2: Close the door for short periods (5–10 minutes) while you're home. Gradually increase.
  • Week 3+: Use the crate for naps and overnight. Never use it as punishment.

The most important rule: Never leave your puppy in the crate longer than they can physically hold their bladder. If you do, they'll have an accident in the crate, which teaches them that soiling their bed is acceptable — completely defeating the purpose.

Crate size also depends on breed. A Chihuahua needs a very different crate than a Labrador retriever. Take our Dog Breed Match Quiz → to find out your puppy's expected adult size and plan your crate accordingly.

Nighttime Potty Strategy

Nighttime is the hardest part because it disrupts your sleep. But there's a predictable pattern, and knowing it makes it bearable.

For 8–12 week old puppies: Set an alarm for 3–4 hours after bedtime. When it goes off, quietly take your puppy out of the crate, carry them to the potty spot, give your cue, and wait. If they go, treat calmly and return to the crate. If they don't go, wait 5 minutes and try again. Under no circumstances should you play, turn on bright lights, or make this fun. This is strictly business.

Progress milestone: At around 4 months, most puppies can stretch 6 hours overnight. By 5 months, many sleep through the night (7–8 hours).

Nighttime tips:

  • Keep the crate in your bedroom or nearby hallway — your puppy will feel more secure hearing you nearby
  • Stop water 2 hours before bedtime
  • Take them out for a final trip right before you go to sleep (even if they're already asleep — wake them gently)
  • If they cry in the crate at night, give them 5 minutes to settle before responding — sometimes they're just vocalizing, not needing to potty

What to Do When Accidents Happen

Accidents will happen. How you handle them determines whether they speed up or slow down the training process.

Step 1: Interrupt, don't punish. If you catch your puppy in the act, make a calm sound ("ah-ah" or clap once) to interrupt them. This should startle them just enough to stop mid-stream, but not scare them.

Step 2: Immediately take them outside. Carry them to their potty spot. If they finish outside, reward them. This connects the dots: "outside is where I'm supposed to do this."

Step 3: Clean thoroughly with enzymatic cleaner. This is not optional. If you clean with anything less, your puppy will return to that spot. Soak the area thoroughly and let it air dry. Shop Nature's Miracle cleaner →

⚠️ Never punish your puppy for accidents. Punishment — yelling, rubbing their nose in it, hitting — doesn't work. It teaches your puppy to fear you and to hide their pottying (behind the couch, under the bed, in another room). This makes accidents harder to catch and clean, and can create long-term anxiety issues. Clean up calmly and adjust your schedule.

Potty Training in Apartments (Without a Yard)

Apartment potty training adds two challenges: the time it takes to get outside, and the lack of an immediate outdoor space. Here's how to handle both.

Use a hybrid pad-to-outdoor approach. Start with puppy pads or a Fresh Patch (real grass tray) on your balcony or in a bathroom. After 1–2 weeks, move the pad progressively closer to the door. Then move it just outside the door. Then to the final potty spot. Each transition should take a few days. This gives your puppy time to adjust without confusion.

Take the elevator, not the stairs. If you live in an apartment building, the time between "I need to go" and "we're at the spot" is your biggest enemy. Carry your puppy down to reduce jostling their bladder. And choose your potty spot strategically — as close to your building's exit as possible.

Go out more frequently. If the rule is "every 60 minutes for a 10-week-old puppy," in an apartment you need to go every 45 minutes. The travel time eats into their available hold time.

Common Potty Training Mistakes

Most potty training problems aren't caused by "difficult" puppies. They're caused by easily fixable owner mistakes. Here are the five most common:

  1. Punishing accidents. Already covered — but it's worth repeating. Punishment creates a fearful dog who hides their accidents, not a house-trained dog.
  2. Crate too large. If your puppy can comfortably eliminate in their crate and sleep in a separate area, the crate is too big. Use the divider.
  3. Using the wrong cleaner. Ammonia-based cleaners (including many floor cleaners) smell like urine to a dog. Stick with enzymatic cleaners exclusively for accident spots.
  4. Inconsistent schedule. Potty training runs on predictability. If feeding times, walk times, and potty breaks vary wildly day to day, your puppy can't build a reliable internal schedule.
  5. Giving too much freedom too soon. Until your puppy has gone 4–6 weeks without an accident, they shouldn't have unsupervised access to the whole house. One room at a time. Manage the environment.

How Long Does Potty Training Take?

Realistic expectations prevent frustration. Here's a typical timeline:

  • 8–16 weeks: Frequent accidents. Your puppy has very little bladder control. Focus on scheduling and management, not "training."
  • 4–6 months: Significant improvement. Your puppy can hold it longer, and most nighttime accidents stop. Expect occasional regression during teething.
  • 6–12 months: Most puppies are fully reliable. Small breeds and stubborn individuals may take longer. Some toy breeds aren't fully reliable until 12–18 months.
  • Factors that affect speed: Breed size (small breeds take longer), consistency of your schedule, whether your puppy was raised in a clean environment before coming home, and your use of crate training.

If your puppy is regressing or not making progress after 2–3 months of consistent training, consult a veterinarian to rule out urinary tract infections or other medical causes.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to potty train a puppy?
Most puppies build reliable habits by 4–6 months of age, with full bladder control by 6–12 months. Small breeds may take up to a year. Consistency with scheduling and crate training significantly speeds up the process.
What's the best way to potty train a puppy in an apartment?
Use a hybrid approach: start with puppy pads on a balcony or bathroom, then gradually move them closer to the door and transition to outdoor-only. You'll need to take your puppy out more frequently since the trip outside takes extra time. A Fresh Patch real grass tray can help bridge the transition. Shop apartment potty training supplies →
Should I use puppy pads or go straight to outdoor training?
If you have a yard, go straight to outdoor training — it's faster and simpler. For apartment dwellers, puppy pads are a practical temporary tool. The key is having a clear phase-out plan: move pads progressively closer to the door, then to the outdoor spot over 2–3 weeks.
How often should I take my puppy out to pee?
As a rule of thumb: one hour per month of age. An 8-week-old needs to go out every 60–90 minutes. A 4-month-old can wait 2–3 hours. Always take them out immediately after waking, within 15 minutes after eating, after play sessions, and before bedtime.
Can I crate train my puppy while I'm at work?
A puppy under 4 months should not stay in a crate longer than 3–4 hours. If you work full-time, arrange for a mid-day visitor or use a playpen with a designated potty pad area. Forcing a puppy to hold it too long leads to crate accidents, which undermines the training completely.
What cleaner gets rid of puppy urine smell?
Enzymatic cleaners like Nature's Miracle are the only products that completely eliminate urine odor. Regular cleaners leave behind scent markers that attract your puppy back to the same spot. Soak accidents thoroughly and let air dry. Shop Nature's Miracle →
Is it normal for puppies to pee in their crate?
No — a properly sized crate should discourage this. If your puppy is peeing in their crate, it's likely too large (they can sleep in one area and eliminate in another) or you're exceeding their bladder capacity. Reduce the space with the divider and adjust your schedule. If problems persist, consult your vet to rule out a UTI.
At what age can a puppy hold its bladder all night?
Most puppies sleep through the night by 4–5 months. At 8–12 weeks, expect 1–2 nighttime potty breaks. Restrict water 2 hours before bed and take them out right before you go to sleep. Setting an alarm is better than waiting for your puppy to cry — be proactive.
