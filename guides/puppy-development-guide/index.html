🐶 Puppy Development Stages: Week-by-Week Guide to Your Puppy's First 20 Weeks

Last updated: June 2026 · Based on American Kennel Club puppy development standards, veterinary growth guidelines, and certified professional trainer recommendations

You just brought home an 8-week-old bundle of teeth, energy, and chaos. Or maybe your dog just had a litter and you're watching tiny newborns squirm without a clue what's normal. Either way, you're asking the same question: what should my puppy be doing at this age — and what comes next?

Puppies develop at an astonishing pace. A newborn that can't see, hear, or stand at week 1 becomes a playful, boundary-testing adolescent by week 20 — that's the equivalent of a human baby becoming a toddler in under five months. Every week brings new milestones, new challenges, and new opportunities to set your puppy up for a lifetime of good behavior.

This guide breaks down exactly what's happening at each stage, what you need to do, what supplies you'll need, and when to call the vet. Whether you're preparing for a new puppy or in the thick of the chaos, bookmark this page — you'll reference it weekly.

Weeks 1–2: Newborn Stage — Helpless but Growing Fast

Weeks 1–2 Newborn

Physical state: Eyes and ears fully closed. Unable to stand, walk, or regulate body temperature. Cannot urinate or defecate without stimulation from the mother.

Growth rate: Weight should double in the first 10–14 days. Daily weighing is the best way to track health.

The first two weeks of a puppy's life are the most vulnerable. Newborns are completely dependent on their mother for warmth, nutrition, and elimination. Their world consists of three things: warmth, milk, and contact.

What's happening physically: Eyes and ear canals are sealed shut. The puppy's nervous system is still developing — the only movements are reflexive: rooting for the nipple, crawling toward warmth, and kneading to stimulate milk flow. Body temperature regulation doesn't kick in until around week 3, so maintaining an ambient temperature of 85–90°F (29–32°C) is critical.

What you should do: Weigh each puppy daily and track progress. Any puppy that isn't gaining weight or is losing weight needs immediate veterinary attention. Keep the whelping box clean and warm. Handle puppies gently and briefly — this early handling has been shown to produce more resilient adult dogs. If you're raising an orphaned litter, formula feeding every 2–3 hours around the clock is necessary.

There are no supplies to buy at this stage beyond what the mother needs. The real shopping starts in a few weeks.

Weeks 3–4: Transitional Stage — Eyes Open, First Steps

Weeks 3–4 Transitional

Milestones: Eyes open (10–14 days), hearing develops, first wobbly steps, tail wagging begins, weaning starts at week 4.

Behavioral shift: Puppies transition from reflexes to intentional movement. They begin interacting with littermates and responding to sounds.

Week 3 is when puppies transform from helpless newborns into actual puppy-shaped creatures. Watching them take their first wobbly steps and respond to sound for the first time is one of the most rewarding parts of raising a litter.

Eyes and ears: Eyes typically open between days 10–14, though one eye may open before the other. Vision is blurry at first and doesn't fully clarify until around week 4. Hearing begins around week 3. Never force a puppy's eyes open — let nature take its course. If eyes haven't opened by day 16, consult a vet.

First steps: Around day 18–21, puppies start trying to stand. They'll be wobbly and fall frequently — that's normal. By week 4, they can walk short distances and even attempt a playful pounce on littermates.

Weaning begins (week 4): This is the first important feeding milestone. Start by mixing high-quality puppy food with puppy formula or warm water to create a soft, oatmeal-like gruel. Place a small amount in a shallow dish — puppies will step in it, wear it, and eventually eat it. Gradually reduce the liquid over 2–3 weeks until they're eating dry kibble by week 7–8. Choose a complete and balanced puppy formula that meets AAFCO nutritional standards.

By the end of week 4, your puppy should be able to walk, see (blurrily), hear, and eat semi-solid food. The adventure is just beginning.

Weeks 5–7: Socialization Window — The Most Critical Period

Weeks 5–7 Socialization

Critical learning: Bite inhibition, littermate social cues, human handling desensitization.

First vet visit: DHPP vaccination at 6–8 weeks. Deworming begins.

This is the most important developmental window in your puppy's life. What happens — or doesn't happen — during weeks 5–7 shapes the adult dog's temperament more than any other period. The science is clear: puppies who receive appropriate socialization during this window grow into more confident, less fearful adults.

Bite inhibition: Between weeks 5 and 7, littermates teach each other how hard is too hard. When one puppy bites another too hard, the victim yelps and stops playing. This feedback loop is how puppies learn to moderate their bite pressure. Puppies removed from their litter before 8 weeks often struggle with bite inhibition throughout their lives.

Human handling: Daily gentle handling — paws, ears, mouth, tail — desensitizes puppies to the touching they'll experience during grooming, vet exams, and everyday life. Puppies not handled during this period are more likely to be anxious about nail trims and ear cleanings as adults.

First vaccination: The first DHPP shot (distemper, hepatitis, parainfluenza, parvovirus) is given at 6–8 weeks. Deworming also begins during this period. After the first shot, your puppy has partial protection but isn't fully immune until the vaccine series is complete — typically around 16 weeks.

Weeks 8–10: Homecoming — The Puppy Arrives

Weeks 8–10 Homecoming

Big changes: Puppy leaves littermates and joins your home. Crate training, potty training, and alone-time training begin.

Second vaccination: DHPP booster at 10–12 weeks plus leptospirosis and bordetella.

This is it — the week your puppy comes home. And it's going to be intense. The first 72 hours set the tone for everything that follows, so preparation is everything.

Before the puppy arrives: Puppy-proof your home by removing or blocking access to electrical cords, toxic plants, small objects that could be swallowed, and anything at chewing height. Set up a designated puppy zone with a crate, bed, water bowl, and potty area. The fewer freedoms your puppy has in the beginning, the fewer mistakes they'll make.

Crate Training — The First Night

The crate should be your puppy's safe space, not a prison. Introduce it positively: toss treats inside, feed meals in the crate, leave the door open initially. For the first few nights, place the crate next to your bed so your puppy can smell and hear you. A ticking clock wrapped in a towel can mimic a heartbeat and soothe anxiety. Expect crying — it's normal. Respond to genuine distress but don't reward every whimper with attention.

Potty Training Begins

At 8–10 weeks, your puppy has very limited bladder control — about 2 hours maximum. Take them out on a fixed schedule: immediately upon waking, after every meal, after play sessions, and before bedtime. Use a consistent verbal cue ("go potty") and reward immediately when they eliminate in the right spot. Accidents inside are your fault, not the puppy's — clean thoroughly with an enzyme cleaner to remove all scent traces.

For apartment dwellers or those without immediate yard access, potty pads are a practical interim solution. Place them in a consistent spot and gradually move them closer to the door over time.

Alone-Time Training — Prevent Separation Anxiety

Apartment dogs are especially prone to separation anxiety because they hear every noise — you leaving, hallway footsteps, elevator dings. Start alone training on day one: leave your puppy alone in their crate for 5 minutes, return calmly, gradually extend the duration. Leave a stuffed Kong or safe chew toy to create positive associations with alone time. Never make a big emotional production of leaving or returning — it signals to your puppy that being alone is a big deal.

By the end of week 10, your puppy should be comfortable in their crate, starting to understand potty training, and learning that alone time is safe and normal.

Weeks 11–14: Teething — The Chewing Frenzy

Weeks 11–14 Teething

Dental: 28 baby teeth begin falling out, 42 adult teeth start coming in. This is the peak chewing period.

Training: Basic obedience — sit, down, stay, leave it. Continued socialization in safe environments.

If your puppy seems possessed by a chewing demon during this period, take a deep breath — it's completely normal. The discomfort of 28 baby teeth being replaced by 42 adult teeth drives puppies to chew anything and everything. Your furniture legs are not safe. Your shoes are not safe. Your baseboards are definitely not safe.

Teething timeline: Incisors fall out around 3–4 months, followed by premolars and canines. By 6 months, all 42 adult teeth should be in. The most intense chewing coincides with the most uncomfortable period — roughly weeks 12–20.

What works: Frozen treats are your best friend. Fill a Kong with plain yogurt or wet puppy food and freeze it — the cold soothes inflamed gums and the chewing satisfies the urge. Freeze a wet washcloth (twisted and frozen) for a low-cost teething toy. Offer a variety of textures: rubber, nylon, rope, and natural chews. Rotate toys to maintain novelty. And absolute rule: never play tug-of-war with a puppy who's actively teething — it can misalign emerging adult teeth.

Basic obedience: Weeks 11–14 are prime time for sit, down, stay, come, and leave it commands. Keep training sessions short (5–10 minutes), use high-value rewards, and end on a positive note. Puppies this age have the attention span of a gnat — work with it, not against it. Leave it is the single most important safety command you'll teach your puppy — it can prevent them from eating something toxic on a walk.

Second vaccination: The DHPP booster at 10–12 weeks adds leptospirosis and bordetella. After this shot, your puppy has stronger protection but still isn't fully covered until the third shot. You can start puppy classes and controlled playdates with vaccinated dogs, but avoid high-traffic public areas until one week after the final shot.

Weeks 15–20: Adolescence — Testing Boundaries

Weeks 15–20 Adolescence

Behavioral shift: "Teenage rebellion" — selective hearing, boundary testing, growth spurts.

Third vaccination: Final DHPP shot + rabies at 14–16 weeks. Full public access 1 week later.

Welcome to the teenage years. Around week 15–16, many puppy owners experience a moment of panic: "My puppy suddenly forgot everything I taught them!" They haven't forgotten — they're testing you. This is a normal developmental phase, and how you respond determines whether it's a passing phase or a permanent behavior pattern.

The "teenage rebellion": Your puppy may ignore commands they previously performed reliably, develop selective hearing on walks, and push boundaries they respected before. Stay consistent. Go back to basics with reward-based training. Increase exercise to channel that adolescent energy. And most importantly: don't take it personally — your puppy is literally going through hormonal and neurological changes that make impulse control difficult.

Growth spurts — feeding adjustments: During rapid growth phases, your puppy's nutritional needs change. Medium and large breed puppies grow fastest between 4–7 months, and their growth plates don't close until 12–18 months. Overfeeding calories or calcium during this period can cause skeletal abnormalities. Feed a high-quality puppy food formulated for your dog's expected adult size, and follow feeding guidelines rather than free-feeding. Use our Pet Food Calculator to determine the exact daily amount based on your puppy's current weight, age, and breed size.

Leash walking and recall: This is the age where loose-leash walking and reliable recall become non-negotiable. Your puppy is stronger, faster, and more curious than they were at 8 weeks. Practice recall in a long-line (15–30 feet) in safe, enclosed areas before attempting off-leash in any unconfined space. The "come" command could save your puppy's life — invest the training time now.

Third vaccination + full freedom: The final DHPP shot and rabies vaccine are given at 14–16 weeks. One week after the third DHPP shot, your puppy is fully protected and can explore public parks, sidewalks, and pet stores safely. This is also the legal requirement for rabies vaccination in most states.

By week 20, your puppy isn't a tiny baby anymore — they're a young adolescent with adult teeth, a developing personality, and growing independence. The intensive puppy stage is winding down, but consistent training continues to shape the adult dog they'll become.

When to Call the Vet — Red Flags by Stage

Not every symptom is an emergency, but knowing which ones require immediate attention can save your puppy's life. Here are the non-negotiable vet visits by developmental stage:

  • Weeks 1–2: Puppy not gaining weight or losing weight. Constant crying (may indicate illness or maternal neglect). Failure to nurse.
  • Weeks 3–4: Eyes haven't started opening by day 16. Extreme difficulty standing or walking. Umbilical hernia (visible bulge at belly button).
  • Weeks 8–10: Diarrhea or vomiting in the first 72 hours home (stress-related or possible parvo). Lethargy + loss of appetite. Coughing or nasal discharge.
  • Any stage: Seizures, difficulty breathing, bloody diarrhea, known toxin ingestion, or any symptom that feels "wrong" — trust your instincts.

If you're ever unsure, call your vet. That's what they're there for. And if you suspect your puppy has eaten something toxic, check our Toxicity Checker while you prepare to head to the clinic.

Frequently Asked Questions

When do puppies open their eyes?
Puppies typically open their eyes between 10–14 days of age. The process is gradual — one eye may open before the other, and vision is blurry at first. Full visual clarity develops around week 4. Never force a puppy's eyes open. If eyes haven't started opening by day 16, consult a veterinarian.
When can puppies go outside for the first time?
Puppies can go into your own backyard for potty training immediately (parvovirus risk is low in private yards). For public spaces like parks and sidewalks, wait until 1 week after the third DHPP vaccination (typically around 16–17 weeks). Before full immunity, carry your puppy to safe areas or use a pet stroller for outdoor exposure.
When do puppies stop biting?
Mouthing and biting peaks during teething (3–5 months) and typically subsides by 6 months once all adult teeth have come in. Consistent bite inhibition training — redirecting to appropriate chew toys and ending play when teeth contact skin — is essential. Puppies who learned bite inhibition from littermates before 8 weeks tend to mouth less as adults.
How long does puppy teething last?
Puppy teething typically lasts from 3 to 6 months of age. The 28 baby teeth begin falling out around 11–12 weeks, and the 42 adult teeth are usually fully in by 6 months. The most intense chewing period is between 12–20 weeks. Providing appropriate chew toys, frozen treats, and teething rings can significantly reduce damage to your belongings.
When do puppies start eating solid food?
Weaning begins around 4 weeks of age. Start by mixing high-quality puppy food with puppy formula or warm water to create a soft gruel. Gradually reduce the liquid over 2–3 weeks. Most puppies are fully weaned onto solid food by 7–8 weeks old. Always choose a complete and balanced puppy formula that meets AAFCO nutritional standards for growth.
What vaccines do puppies need and when?
The core puppy vaccination schedule includes: DHPP first shot at 6–8 weeks, DHPP second shot at 10–12 weeks, DHPP third shot at 14–16 weeks, and rabies vaccine at 14–16 weeks (legally required). Optional vaccines include leptospirosis, bordetella (kennel cough), and canine influenza based on your location and your dog's lifestyle.
When do puppies sleep through the night?
Most puppies start sleeping through the night (6–8 hours) around 16–20 weeks of age. At 8–10 weeks, expect 1–2 nighttime potty breaks. Strategies that help: consistent bedtime routine, crate training, limiting water 1 hour before bed, and a final potty break before lights out. Puppies under 12 weeks physically cannot hold their bladder all night.
Is pet insurance worth it for a puppy?
Absolutely — insuring your puppy early is the smartest financial move in pet ownership. Puppy insurance covers accidents (broken bones, ingestion), illnesses (parvo, kennel cough), and if you enroll early, many policies cover hereditary conditions like hip dysplasia that would be excluded as pre-existing conditions later.
