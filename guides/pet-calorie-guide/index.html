⚡ How Many Calories Does My Dog Need? Daily Calorie Guide

Last updated: June 2026 · Based on NRC (National Research Council) veterinary formulas

Just like humans, every dog has a unique daily calorie requirement. A sedentary 10 lb Chihuahua needs vastly fewer calories than an active 70 lb Labrador Retriever. Feeding the wrong amount can lead to weight gain, malnutrition, or energy imbalances.

This guide explains RER vs. DER, how to calculate your dog's exact calorie needs, and what factors change the numbers.

⚡ Quick Answer: Most adult neutered dogs need about 60–70 calories per kg of body weight daily. A 25 kg (55 lb) neutered dog needs ~1,100 kcal/day. Active dogs need more; overweight dogs need less. Use our Pet Calorie Calculator → for a precise number.

RER vs. DER: What's the Difference?

Veterinarians use two key terms when calculating calorie needs:

RER — Resting Energy Requirement

RER is the number of calories your pet needs for basic bodily functions at rest — breathing, heartbeat, brain function, digestion. Think of it as your pet's "idle" energy cost. The formula is:

RER = 70 × (weight in kg)0.75

This formula is the same for both dogs and cats. A 25 kg dog has an RER of about 70 × 250.75 ≈ 70 × 11.2 ≈ 784 kcal/day.

DER — Daily Energy Requirement

DER is RER multiplied by a factor that accounts for your pet's real-life situation — age, activity level, neuter status, and body condition. This is the number you actually feed to.

Weight Management: When to Adjust Calories

If your dog is overweight:

Reduce to the "overweight/sedentary" multiplier (1.3× RER). Switch to a weight management formula food, measure portions precisely (use a kitchen scale, not just a cup), and increase exercise gradually. A healthy weight loss rate is 1–2% of body weight per week.

If your dog is underweight:

Increase to the "active" or "intact" multiplier. Consider a higher-calorie food, add healthy toppers, and feed smaller more frequent meals. Consult your vet to rule out medical causes first — unexplained weight loss can indicate parasites, thyroid issues, or other conditions.

For puppies:

Puppies need 2–3× their RER because they're growing rapidly. Feed puppy-specific formula and monitor body condition weekly. Adjust portions as they grow — a 4-month-old puppy needs very different amounts than the same dog at 10 months.

How Many Calories Are in Dog Food?

Knowing your dog's calorie needs is only half the equation — you also need to know your food's calorie density. Typical values:

  • Dry kibble: 350–450 kcal per cup (varies by brand and formula)
  • Wet food: 250–350 kcal per 13 oz can; ~100–150 kcal per 3 oz can
  • Semi-moist food: 300–400 kcal per cup
  • Treats: 10–50 kcal each (account for no more than 10% of daily calories)

Always check the label! Some "light" formulas have 300 kcal/cup while high-performance foods can exceed 450 kcal/cup. A 10% difference in calorie density can mean 100+ extra calories per day for a medium-sized dog — enough to cause weight gain over months.

Cats: Special Considerations

Cats generally need fewer calories than dogs of the same weight. A neutered adult 5 kg (11 lb) cat needs about 220–260 kcal/day — roughly the same as a 5 lb dog. Cats are also more prone to obesity, especially indoor-only cats. Use our Pet Calorie Calculator (it handles cats too) and consider a weight management cat food if needed.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many calories does a 50 lb dog need per day?
A neutered, moderately active 50 lb (22.7 kg) dog needs approximately 1,100–1,200 kcal per day. An intact or very active dog of the same weight may need 1,500–1,850 kcal. Use our Pet Calorie Calculator for your dog's exact needs based on age and body condition.
How many cups of food should I feed my dog per day?
For a typical dry food (~375 kcal/cup), a 30 lb dog needs 1½–2 cups, a 50 lb dog needs 2–3 cups, and a 70 lb dog needs 2¾–3¾ cups per day. Check your specific food's calorie density — premium foods often have more calories per cup, meaning smaller portions.
What's the best way to help my dog lose weight?
Calculate their actual calorie needs (our calculator does this), reduce portions by 10–20%, switch to a weight management formula, measure food with a scale (not a cup), cut treats by half, and increase exercise gradually. Aim for 1–2% body weight loss per week. Shop weight management dog food →
Do neutered dogs need fewer calories?
Yes. Neutered/spayed dogs need about 20–30% fewer calories than intact dogs of the same weight. Hormonal changes after neutering reduce metabolic rate and increase appetite. If you don't adjust portions after spaying/neutering, weight gain is almost guaranteed. Our calculator accounts for this automatically.
How many calories does my cat need vs. my dog?
Cats need fewer calories than dogs of the same weight. A 5 kg (11 lb) neutered cat needs ~220–260 kcal/day, while a 5 kg dog needs ~350–400 kcal/day. Cats also have different nutritional requirements — they need more protein and specific amino acids (taurine) that dogs don't require in the same amounts.
