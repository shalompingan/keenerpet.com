🐕 Best Dogs for Apartments: Top 10 Breeds & Complete Guide (2026)

Last updated: June 2026 · Based on American Kennel Club breed standards, veterinary behavior guidelines, and owner experience data

You're looking at your 650-square-foot apartment and wondering: can I actually have a dog here? It's the most common question from city-dwelling dog lovers — and the answer is almost always yes — if you choose the right breed.

Over 40% of dogs in the United States live in apartments or multi-family housing. The difference between a happy apartment dog and a stressed one isn't square footage — it's breed selection, exercise commitment, and smart preparation. A French Bulldog in a studio apartment can thrive while a Border Collie in a 2,000-square-foot house can be miserable if its needs aren't met.

This guide ranks the 10 best apartment dog breeds based on temperament, energy levels, barking tendencies, and adaptability to small spaces. We'll also cover the five factors you need to evaluate before choosing a breed, the essential supplies every apartment dog owner needs, and how to budget realistically for apartment dog ownership.

5 Factors to Consider Before Getting a Dog in an Apartment

Before we get to the rankings, let's talk about what actually makes a dog suitable for apartment living. These five factors matter more than any breed list — get them right, and your apartment dog situation will work. Get them wrong, and you'll be rehoming your dog in six months.

① Space — Big Dogs in Small Spaces

The biggest myth in dog ownership: big dogs need big spaces. In reality, an underexercised small dog is far more destructive than a well-exercised large one. A Greyhound — 60–80 pounds of racing dog — is often called the "45-mile-per-hour couch potato" because they're perfectly content sleeping 18 hours a day. Meanwhile, a Jack Russell Terrier with insufficient exercise will redecorate your apartment with chewed baseboards.

The real question isn't "how big is your apartment?" — it's "how much time can you commit to daily exercise?" That's the single biggest factor determining whether a breed will work in your space.

② Noise & Barking — Keeping the Peace

This is the #1 concern for apartment dwellers, and for good reason. Noise complaints are the leading cause of pet restrictions in rental buildings. Some breeds are naturally quiet — French Bulldogs, Cavaliers, and Shih Tzus rarely bark without reason. Others — Beagles, Huskies, and Miniature Pinschers — are vocal by nature and will test your neighbor's patience.

But here's what most guides won't tell you: barking is usually a symptom, not a personality flaw. Separation anxiety, boredom, and insufficient exercise cause far more barking than breed disposition. A tired, mentally stimulated dog of any breed is a quiet dog.

③ Energy Levels — Match Your Lifestyle

This is where most breed-selection mistakes happen. People fall in love with a breed's look without understanding its energy needs. Here's a simple framework:

  • Low energy (30 min/day): French Bulldog, Shih Tzu, Pug, Bulldog, Greyhound
  • Moderate energy (45–60 min/day): Cavalier King Charles Spaniel, Bichon Frise, Havanese, Maltese
  • High energy (60+ min/day): Beagle, Miniature Schnauzer, Pomeranian, Corgi

Be brutally honest with yourself about how much time you can realistically dedicate to walking and play. If you work 9–6 and value your evenings on the couch, a low-energy breed isn't a compromise — it's the right match.

④ Breed Restrictions — What Your Lease Says

Before you fall in love with a breed, check your lease. Many apartment complexes maintain restricted breed lists that typically include Pit Bulls, German Shepherds, Rottweilers, Dobermans, Huskies, Chow Chows, and Akitas. Some also enforce weight limits (under 25–40 lbs), which eliminates many medium and large breeds regardless of temperament.

If you're planning to move soon, factor this into your breed decision. And if you already have a restricted breed, consider renter's insurance that covers dog liability — some landlords will waive restrictions if you carry appropriate coverage.

⑤ Budget — The Real Cost of Apartment Dog Ownership

Dogs in apartments come with unique costs beyond the standard food and vet care. Here's what to expect:

  • Pet deposit: $200–$500 (often non-refundable)
  • Monthly pet rent: $25–$75 on top of your regular rent
  • Dog walking services: $15–$30 per walk, potentially $200–$400/month if you work full-time
  • Professional training: $150–$300 for group classes (essential for apartment manners)
  • Emergency vet fund: This is where apartment dogs are uniquely vulnerable — ingestion accidents in small spaces are common, and the costs add up fast

Top 10 Best Dog Breeds for Apartments

These rankings are based on five criteria: barking tendency, energy level, adaptability to small spaces, temperament with neighbors/strangers, and suitability for first-time owners. Each breed on this list is a proven apartment companion — but they have different personalities and needs, so read the profiles carefully to find your match.

1 French Bulldog

Low Energy Low Barking Great for First-Timers Small (16–28 lbs)

The undisputed king of apartment dogs. French Bulldogs are compact, quiet, and have energy levels that match a Netflix marathon. They rarely bark without reason, don't need a yard, and are content with a 20-minute walk and some indoor play. Their affectionate, people-pleasing temperament makes them excellent companions for singles, couples, and families alike.

One thing to know: Frenchies are brachycephalic (flat-faced), which means they don't tolerate heat well and can have respiratory issues. They're also prone to allergies and skin fold infections — budget for potential veterinary dermatology visits.

2 Cavalier King Charles Spaniel

Moderate Energy Low Barking Great for First-Timers Small (13–18 lbs)

Cavaliers were literally bred to be companion dogs — they excel at exactly what apartment dwellers need. They're gentle, quiet, and adapt seamlessly to small spaces. Their moderate energy means they enjoy daily walks but don't demand constant activity. Cavaliers are famously good with children, other dogs, and strangers, making them ideal for social apartment buildings.

One thing to know: Cavaliers are prone to mitral valve disease (heart condition), so regular vet checkups and pet insurance are especially important for this breed. They also need moderate grooming — their silky coats require brushing 2–3 times per week.

3 Bichon Frise

Moderate Energy Low Shedding Hypoallergenic Small (12–18 lbs)

If allergies are a concern, the Bichon Frise is your top apartment pick. These cheerful, cotton-ball dogs are hypoallergenic (low dander), virtually non-shedding, and have a cheerful disposition that makes them wonderful apartment companions. They're smart, trainable, and get along with everyone — neighbors, visitors, delivery people, and other dogs.

One thing to know: Bichons require consistent grooming — professional visits every 4–6 weeks at $60–$100 per session. Factor that into your budget. They can also develop separation anxiety if left alone for long hours, so consider doggy daycare or a walker if you work full-time outside the home.

4 Shih Tzu

Low Energy Low Barking Great for First-Timers Small (9–16 lbs)

Shih Tzus were literally bred as indoor palace companions in ancient China — apartment living is what they were designed for. They're affectionate, low-energy, and perfectly content being lap dogs. A short daily walk plus indoor play is sufficient exercise. Their small size and quiet nature make them ideal for studio and one-bedroom apartments.

One thing to know: That luxurious coat requires serious grooming — daily brushing and professional grooming every 4–6 weeks. Many owners opt for a "puppy cut" (short all over) to reduce maintenance. Shih Tzus are also brachycephalic, so watch for breathing issues in hot weather.

5 Pug

Low Energy Low Barking Great for First-Timers Small (14–18 lbs)

Pugs are the comedians of the dog world — charming, affectionate, and perpetually happy. They're low-energy, quiet, and adapt beautifully to apartment life. Pugs bond closely with their owners and are content to follow you from room to room (in a 650-square-foot apartment, that's easy). Their short coat means minimal shedding compared to other breeds.

One thing to know: Pugs snore. Loudly. If you're a light sleeper, this matters. They're also prone to obesity (because they love food and hate exercise), so portion control is critical. 6 Maltese

Low Energy Low Shedding Hypoallergenic Toy (4–7 lbs)

The Maltese is the ultimate small-space breed for owners who want a tiny, affectionate companion. At 4–7 pounds, they take up virtually no space, have low exercise requirements (short walks suffice), and are hypoallergenic with minimal shedding. They're gentle, adaptable, and bond deeply with their owners — perfect for singles and seniors in apartments.

One thing to know: Their small size means they can be prone to injury in households with very young children or larger pets. They also need regular grooming and can be challenging to house-train (common with toy breeds). Patience with potty training is essential.

7 Havanese

Moderate Energy Low Shedding Great for First-Timers Small (7–13 lbs)

Havanese dogs are intelligent, adaptable, and thrive on human companionship — exactly what makes a great apartment dog. They're small but sturdy, moderately active without being hyperactive, and their low-shedding coat is a bonus for allergy sufferers. Havanese are known for being quick learners, which makes apartment manners (quiet in hallways, good elevator behavior) much easier to teach.

One thing to know: These dogs form incredibly strong bonds with their owners and can develop separation anxiety if left alone for long stretches. They're ideal for remote workers, retirees, or owners who can arrange midday dog care. Their coat requires brushing 3–4 times per week and professional grooming every 6–8 weeks.

8 Bulldog

Low Energy Low Barking Great for First-Timers Medium (40–50 lbs)

Bulldogs are the heavyweights of the apartment dog world. Despite their size, they're one of the least active breeds — a 15-minute walk is legitimately enough exercise. They're quiet, gentle, and incredibly loyal. Their low energy and calm temperament make them surprisingly well-suited to apartments, even though they're larger than most dogs on this list.

One thing to know: Check your apartment's weight limit before falling for a Bulldog — many buildings cap pets at 25–40 lbs, and adult Bulldogs typically exceed that. They're also prone to skin fold dermatitis, cherry eye, and hip dysplasia. Pet insurance is strongly recommended for this breed. They also drool and snore — if you're a neat freak, this may not be the breed for you.

9 Miniature Schnauzer

Moderate Energy Low Shedding Hypoallergenic Small (11–20 lbs)

Miniature Schnauzers pack a big personality into a compact frame. They're alert, intelligent, and moderately active — they need daily walks and mental stimulation but are calm indoors when their needs are met. Their wire coat is low-shedding and hypoallergenic. They make excellent watchdogs (they'll alert you to hallway activity) without being nuisance barkers if properly trained.

One thing to know: Mini Schnauzers are clever and can be stubborn. They need consistent training and mental enrichment — puzzle toys and nose work games are excellent. Without enough stimulation, they can become destructive. They also need professional grooming every 5–8 weeks for that distinctive wiry coat.

10 Greyhound

Low Energy (Indoors) Low Barking Good for First-Timers Large (50–80 lbs)

The surprise entry on this list — and perhaps the most misunderstood apartment dog. Greyhounds are sprinters, not endurance athletes. On the track, they explode for 30 seconds. In your apartment, they sleep 18–20 hours a day. They're famously gentle, quiet, and clean indoors. Retired racing Greyhounds are often already crate-trained and housebroken, making them one of the easiest adult dogs to adopt.

One thing to know: Size matters for apartment living. At 50–80 pounds, a Greyhound needs floor space to stretch out — they'll claim your sofa and bed without hesitation. Check for weight limits in your lease. They also have thin skin that tears easily and can be sensitive to cold weather. Adopting a retired racer is an incredibly rewarding experience that also gives a deserving dog a second chance.

Apartment Dog Essentials — What You'll Need

Once you've chosen your breed, the right gear makes apartment living with a dog dramatically easier. Here's what apartment dwellers should prioritize.

Crate & Bed — Space-Saving Solutions

In an apartment, multi-functional is better. Look for a crate that doubles as a side table (many wire crates come with flat tops that work as surfaces), or a furniture-style crate that blends with your decor. For beds, don't buy a large bed you don't have floor space for — get one that fits your dog's sleeping style (bolster beds for dogs who like to lean, flat mats for sprawlers).

Potty Solutions for High-Rise Living

If you live above the 3rd floor, you need a backup plan for late-night or bad-weather potty breaks. Real grass patch systems (like Fresh Patch or Bark Potty) deliver natural sod to your door and are surprisingly effective. Reusable artificial grass patches with drainage trays are a more economical long-term option. For puppy owners, potty pads in a designated area are essential during the training phase.

Exercise & Mental Stimulation

Without a yard, you need creative ways to burn energy. Snuffle mats and puzzle toys provide mental stimulation that's often more tiring than physical exercise. For rainy days, indoor fetch with soft toys or flirt poles work in hallways and living rooms. And if your schedule allows, a midday dog walker is worth every penny — sites like Rover connect you with local walkers starting around $15–20 per walk.

Common Apartment Dog Challenges — Solved

Separation Anxiety in Small Spaces

Apartment dogs are uniquely prone to separation anxiety because they're constantly exposed to hallway noises, neighbor footsteps, and the sounds of you leaving. The solution: start with micro-absences (2–5 minutes) and gradually build up. Leave a stuffed Kong or puzzle toy when you leave. Consider a white noise machine or "dog TV" (YouTube has hours of content designed for dogs) to mask triggering sounds. In severe cases, consult a veterinary behaviorist — some dogs benefit from medication during the training process.

Potty Training Without a Yard

Potty training in an apartment takes more consistency than in a house with a yard. You can't just open a door — you need to leash up, go down the elevator, and walk to a designated spot. The key: establish a fixed route and schedule from day one. Take your puppy out on a consistent schedule (first thing, after meals, after naps, every 2 hours). Reward immediately at the potty spot. With consistency, most dogs adapt within 2–4 weeks.

Managing Encounters in Shared Spaces

Elevators, stairwells, and hallways are your dog's equivalent of busy intersections. Train your dog to sit and wait before entering and exiting the elevator — this prevents door-dashing and sets a calm tone. In hallways, keep your dog close to one side and use a short leash when passing other residents. A "say hello" cue (only greet when you explicitly release) prevents reactive behavior.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best small dog for an apartment?
French Bulldogs, Cavalier King Charles Spaniels, Bichon Frises, and Shih Tzus consistently rank as the best small apartment dogs. They have low to moderate energy levels, minimal barking tendencies, and adapt well to small living spaces. The best choice depends on your specific lifestyle — our Dog Breed Match quiz can help you find the perfect fit.
Can a large dog live in an apartment?
Yes — many large breeds adapt well to apartment life provided they get sufficient daily exercise. Greyhounds, Bulldogs, and even Great Danes are known to be surprisingly calm indoors. The key factor is energy level and temperament, not size alone. A well-exercised large dog is often calmer indoors than an underexercised small breed like a Jack Russell Terrier.
What is the quietest dog breed for apartments?
French Bulldogs, Bulldogs, Cavalier King Charles Spaniels, Greyhounds, and Shih Tzus are among the quietest breeds for apartment living. These breeds have naturally low barking tendencies and are calm indoors. Note that individual temperament varies — proper training and adequate exercise are essential regardless of breed.
How much exercise does an apartment dog need?
Exercise needs vary significantly by breed. Low-energy breeds like French Bulldogs and Shih Tzus need about 30 minutes of daily walking. Moderate-energy breeds like Cavaliers and Havanese need 45–60 minutes. High-energy breeds kept in apartments require at least 60–90 minutes of vigorous exercise plus mental stimulation through puzzle toys and training.
Is it cruel to keep a dog in an apartment?
No — apartment living is not inherently cruel for dogs. What matters is whether the dog's exercise, mental stimulation, and social needs are met. Many apartment dogs are happier than yard-kept dogs because they get more daily walks, social interaction, and quality time with their owners. The breed choice, exercise commitment, and your lifestyle are the deciding factors — not square footage.
What dog breeds are usually banned by apartments?
The most commonly restricted breeds include Pit Bulls, German Shepherds, Rottweilers, Doberman Pinschers, Huskies, Chow Chows, and Akitas. Some apartments also enforce weight limits (typically under 25–40 lbs). Always check your lease and local pet policies before getting a dog — breed restrictions vary significantly by property management company and insurance policy.
How do I stop my apartment dog from barking?
Address the root cause first — barking is usually a symptom of boredom, insufficient exercise, or separation anxiety. Increase daily walks, provide mental stimulation through puzzle toys and snuffle mats, establish a consistent routine, and practice desensitization to common hallway and street noises. For severe cases, consult a professional trainer who specializes in apartment settings.
Should I get pet insurance for my apartment dog?
Yes — pet insurance is especially valuable for apartment dogs. They're prone to ingestion accidents (eating toxic plants, chocolate, or objects left within reach in small spaces), and emergency vet bills can reach $800–$3,000+. Apartment dwellers also face higher risks of escape-related injuries. Accident-only plans start around $15–30/month and cover most emergencies. Compare pet insurance plans →
