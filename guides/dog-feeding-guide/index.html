🍽️ How Much to Feed Your Dog? Complete Feeding Guide by Weight & Age

Last updated: June 2026 · Based on NRC nutrient requirements and WSAVA feeding guidelines

Is your dog a picky eater who leaves half their bowl untouched? Or the opposite — a bottomless pit who acts starving an hour after every meal? Maybe you're simply not sure if those "feed X cups" instructions on the bag are right for your dog.

Here's the reality check: the feeding guidelines on dog food bags are designed for the average dog — and your dog isn't average. They don't account for metabolism, activity level, spay/neuter status, or body condition. Feed by the bag and you risk overfeeding (weight gain, joint stress) or underfeeding (low energy, muscle loss).

This guide covers exactly how much to feed your dog based on weight, life stage, and food type — plus the feeding schedule that works best, how to tell if portions are right, and the most common mistakes to avoid.

Feeding by Life Stage

A puppy's nutritional needs are completely different from a senior's. Feeding the wrong balance at any stage can cause long-term health problems.

Puppies (up to 12 months)

Puppies grow at an astonishing rate. A large breed puppy can gain 100+ times their birth weight in their first year. That growth requires energy — about 2–3 times the calories per pound compared to a same-weight adult.

Puppy feeding guidelines:

  • Small breeds (adult weight up to 20 lbs): ½–1 cup per day, split into 3 meals until 6 months, then 2 meals
  • Medium breeds (20–50 lbs): 1–2 cups per day, split into 3 meals until 6 months
  • Large breeds (50–90 lbs): 2–3 cups per day, split into 3 meals — use large breed puppy formula to control growth rate
  • Giant breeds (90+ lbs): 3–4½ cups per day, split into 3 meals — large breed puppy formula is essential

⚠️ Critical for large/giant breed puppies: Feeding too much calcium or too many calories can cause developmental orthopedic disease (hip dysplasia, OCD). Always use a large-breed-specific puppy food and follow portion guidelines precisely. Shop large breed puppy food →

Switch to adult food when your puppy reaches roughly 90% of their expected adult weight — around 12 months for small breeds, 18–24 months for large/giant breeds. Use our Pet Age Calculator to track your puppy's development stage.

Adult Dogs (1–7 years, varies by breed)

Most adult dogs thrive on 2 measured meals per day. The exact amount depends on more than just weight:

  • Activity level: A couch potato needs ~20% fewer calories than an active dog of the same weight
  • Spay/neuter status: Neutered dogs need ~20–30% fewer calories than intact dogs — hormonal changes reduce metabolic rate
  • Body condition: If you can't feel ribs, reduce portions. If ribs are too prominent, increase portions

A neutered, moderately active 50 lb dog needs about 900–1,100 kcal/day. An intact working dog of the same weight may need 1,500+ kcal/day. Use our Pet Calorie Calculator for a precise recommendation based on your dog's specific profile.

Senior Dogs (7+ years, earlier for large breeds)

Senior dogs face a metabolic double-whammy: less activity and a slower metabolism. Most need 15–25% fewer calories than their adult prime. But cutting calories isn't the whole story — they also need higher-quality protein to offset age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia).

Switch to a senior formula that includes:

  • Glucosamine and chondroitin for joint health
  • Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA) for cognitive function and inflammation control
  • High-quality protein to maintain lean muscle mass
  • Lower phosphorus to protect aging kidneys

Wet vs Dry vs Raw Dog Food — What's Best for Your Dog?

One of the most common questions we get: "Should I feed kibble, wet food, or go raw?" The honest answer is that each has trade-offs, and the best choice depends on your dog's health, your budget, and your lifestyle.

Cups to Grams Conversion

Measuring dog food by volume (cups) is convenient but inaccurate. A standard cup of dry kibble weighs anywhere from 100–130 grams depending on kibble size and density. Over time, that 15–30% variance adds up to real calories.

Weighing your dog's food on a kitchen scale is the most accurate method. Here's a rough conversion guide for reference:

Cups Grams (approx) Calories (at 375 kcal/cup)
¼ cup25–33 g~94 kcal
½ cup50–65 g~188 kcal
1 cup100–130 g~375 kcal
1½ cups150–195 g~563 kcal
2 cups200–260 g~750 kcal
3 cups300–390 g~1,125 kcal

A 10% overestimate per cup (from scooped vs level cups) can mean hundreds of extra calories per month — enough for gradual but steady weight gain. If you're serious about portion accuracy, a digital kitchen scale pays for itself in kibble savings alone.

How Often Should You Feed Your Dog?

Veterinarians overwhelmingly recommend 2 meals per day for adult dogs. Here's why the schedule matters more than you might think:

  • Better digestion: Smaller portions are easier to digest than one large meal. The digestive tract processes food more efficiently in two passes.
  • Steady energy: Two meals provide stable blood sugar throughout the day — no post-meal energy crash.
  • Bloat prevention: Feeding one large meal increases the risk of gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV), a life-threatening condition where the stomach twists on itself. This is especially critical for large, deep-chested breeds like Great Danes, German Shepherds, and Standard Poodles.
  • Routine and behavior: Dogs thrive on predictability. A consistent feeding schedule helps regulate bathroom breaks and reduces food-related anxiety.

⚠️ GDV (Bloat) Warning: If you own a large or deep-chested breed, feed 2–3 smaller meals rather than one large meal. Avoid vigorous exercise 1 hour before and 2 hours after meals. GDV has a 20–40% mortality rate even with treatment. Prevention is everything.

If your dog is a picky eater who walks away from meals, try adding a natural food topper or warm water to increase aroma and palatability.

Signs You're Feeding the Right Amount

Numbers on a chart are a starting point. Your dog's body condition is the real test. Here's how to assess it:

🔴 Feeding too much:

  • Ribs are hard to feel under a layer of fat
  • No visible waist when viewed from above (looks like a potato)
  • Belly sags with no abdominal tuck
  • Reluctance to exercise, tires easily

🟢 Feeding the right amount:

  • Ribs can be felt with a light layer of fat (like feeling the back of your hand)
  • Visible waist behind the ribs when viewed from above
  • Abdominal tuck when viewed from the side
  • Consistent energy levels throughout the day

🔴 Feeding too little:

  • Ribs, spine, or hip bones are visibly prominent from a distance
  • Extreme waist tuck from above (hourglass shape)
  • Lethargy or low energy
  • Constant scavenging, begging, or food obsession

5 Common Dog Feeding Mistakes (And How to Fix Them)

These are the most frequent feeding errors we see — and each one is easy to fix once you know about it:

  1. Free-feeding (leaving food out all day). You can't monitor how much your dog actually eats, and you won't notice early appetite loss — the first sign of many illnesses. Fix: Switch to scheduled, measured meals.
  2. Using the food bag's "recommended serving" as gospel. That recommendation is for the average dog on average activity. It doesn't know your dog. Fix: Use the bag as a starting point, then adjust based on body condition.
  3. Scooping kibble with a regular cup. A scoop from a 4-cup measuring cup is rarely accurate. Kibble size and settling mean you could be off by 20–30%. Fix: Use a standard measuring cup or a digital scale.
  4. Treats not accounted for. If you're feeding 2 cups of kibble plus 10 training treats and a dental chew, your dog is getting 15–25% more calories than you think. Fix: Treats should make up no more than 10% of daily calories. Count them.
  5. Switching food too fast. Changing your dog's food overnight causes digestive upset — vomiting, diarrhea, and gas. Fix: Transition over 7–10 days, mixing increasing amounts of new food with decreasing amounts of old food.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I feed my dog once or twice a day?
Twice a day is strongly recommended for most adult dogs. Two meals spaced 8–12 hours apart aids digestion, provides steady energy, and reduces the risk of bloat (GDV) in large, deep-chested breeds. Puppies under 6 months need 3–4 meals daily.
How do I know if I'm feeding my dog enough?
Check their body condition: you should be able to feel their ribs with a light layer of fat (like feeling the back of your hand), see a visible waist from above, and an abdominal tuck from the side. If ribs are too prominent, increase portions. If you can't feel ribs at all, reduce portions.
How many cups of food should I feed my dog?
It depends on your dog's weight and the food's calorie density. A 30 lb dog typically needs 1½–2 cups of dry food per day, a 50 lb dog needs 2–3 cups, and a 70 lb dog needs 2¾–3¾ cups. Always check the kcal/cup on your specific bag and use our Pet Food Calculator for a personalized recommendation.
Is it better to free-feed or schedule meals?
Scheduled, portion-controlled meals are far better than free-feeding. Free-feeding makes it impossible to track how much your dog actually eats, leads to obesity, and means you won't notice early if your dog loses their appetite — which is often the first sign of illness. Stick to measured meals at set times.
Can I mix wet and dry dog food?
Yes! Mixing wet and dry food is a great way to add variety and increase your dog's water intake. Replace about ½ cup of dry food with one 3 oz can (about ¼ cup) of wet food. Wet food is ~70–80% water, so it's less calorie-dense. Adjust dry food portions down to compensate. Shop wet dog food →
How much should I feed a puppy vs an adult dog?
Puppies need 2–3 times the calories per pound of an adult dog. Feed a puppy-specific formula split into 3–4 meals daily until 6 months, then transition to 2 meals. Large and giant breed puppies must eat large-breed-specific formulas to control growth rate. Switch to adult food when your dog reaches 90% of expected adult weight.
What's the best dog food brand for sensitive stomachs?
For sensitive stomachs, look for limited ingredient foods with a single protein source and easily digestible carbohydrates. Purina Pro Plan Sensitive Skin & Stomach, Hill's Science Diet Sensitive Stomach, and Blue Buffalo Basics are top choices. Avoid high-fat foods and artificial additives. Always transition over 7–10 days. Shop sensitive stomach dog food →
Should I feed my senior dog a different diet?
Yes, senior dogs (7+ years, 5–6 for large breeds) benefit from senior-specific food. They need 15–25% fewer calories due to slower metabolism, plus added glucosamine for joints, omega-3s for cognitive health, and high-quality protein to prevent muscle loss.
