👂 Dog Ear Infection Guide: Symptoms, Causes & Treatment

Last updated: June 2026 · Based on veterinary otology research and clinical guidelines

If your dog has ever woken you up at 2 AM scratching an ear raw, you know the drill. You flip on the light, take a look inside, and — that smell hits you. Musty, a little sweet, unmistakably wrong. Congrats, you've just met one of the most common reasons dogs see the vet.

Ear infections (vets call it otitis externa) account for roughly 10–15% of all vet visits in dogs. They're uncomfortable, they're stubborn, and if you've dealt with them before, you know they have a nasty habit of coming back. But here's the good news: once you understand what's actually causing them, most ear infections are entirely preventable or manageable at home.

This guide covers everything you need — how to spot an infection early, what's really going on inside that L-shaped ear canal, which products actually help, and when you absolutely need a vet. Not a single Q-tip recommendation in sight.

What Is a Dog Ear Infection?

A dog ear infection is exactly what it sounds like: inflammation and microbial overgrowth inside the ear canal. But because of how a dog's ear is built, these infections behave very differently than they do in humans.

Dogs have an L-shaped ear canal — a vertical drop followed by a horizontal bend leading to the eardrum. That 90-degree angle is great for amplifying sound but terrible for drainage. Moisture, wax, and debris get trapped in that horizontal canal, creating a warm, dark breeding ground for bacteria and yeast. Once an infection takes hold, that shape also makes it difficult for topical treatments to reach deep enough without proper technique.

There are three levels of ear infections:

  • Otitis Externa — Infection of the outer ear canal (most common, easiest to treat)
  • Otitis Media — Infection spreads to the middle ear (more serious, usually follows chronic externa)
  • Otitis Interna — Infection reaches the inner ear (rare but serious; affects balance and hearing)

This guide focuses on otitis externa, which is what most dog owners will encounter. If your dog is showing balance issues or head tilting, skip straight to when to see a vet.

7 Warning Signs of an Ear Infection in Dogs

Catching an ear infection early makes treatment faster, cheaper, and less painful for your dog. Here's what to look for — check your dog's ears weekly, and definitely check if you notice any of these:

  1. Head shaking or tilting — The most obvious early sign. If your dog is shaking their head more than usual, something feels wrong inside.
  2. Scratching or pawing at the ear — Dogs will scratch with a hind leg or rub their head along the floor or furniture. Raw, red skin around the ear opening is a giveaway.
  3. Redness and swelling — A healthy ear canal should be pale pink. Bright red or swollen tissue means active inflammation.
  4. Foul odor — A musty, yeasty, or sweet smell is characteristic of yeast infections. A foul, rotten smell often points to bacteria like Pseudomonas or Staphylococcus.
  5. Discharge — Brown wax is normal in small amounts. Dark brown/black coffee-ground discharge suggests mites. Yellow or green pus means bacterial infection.
  6. Pain or sensitivity — If your dog flinches, whimpers, or pulls away when you touch their ears, that's pain. Don't force the examination.
  7. Loss of balance or circling — This signals a deeper infection affecting the inner ear. Seek veterinary care immediately.

What Causes Dog Ear Infections? (And Why They Keep Coming Back)

Here's the most important thing to understand: ear infections are almost always a symptom of something else. Treat the infection without addressing the root cause, and it will return. Period.

Allergies — The #1 Root Cause

Up to 90% of recurrent ear infections in dogs are linked to underlying allergies. Environmental allergies (pollen, dust mites, mold) and food allergies (chicken, beef, dairy, wheat are the top triggers) both cause low-grade inflammation in the skin — including the skin lining the ear canal. Inflamed tissue produces more wax and moisture, and that's exactly what yeast and bacteria need to overgrow.

If your dog has recurrent ear infections, seasonal itching, or persistent paw licking, allergies are almost certainly the driver. Managing the allergy — through immunotherapy, hypoallergenic diets, or medication — is what stops the cycle. Moisture Trapped in the Ear

Dogs who swim frequently, get bathed often, or live in humid environments are at higher risk. Water trapped in that L-shaped canal creates the perfect environment for Malassezia yeast and bacteria to flourish. Always dry your dog's ears thoroughly after water exposure.

Ear Mites

Ear mites (Otodectes cynotis) are highly contagious and most common in puppies and outdoor dogs. They cause intense itching and produce a distinctive dark, crumbly discharge that looks like coffee grounds. Mites require specific antiparasitic treatment — regular ear cleaners won't get rid of them.

Anatomical Predisposition

Some dogs are simply built for ear infections. Floppy ears (like Cocker Spaniels and Labradors) reduce airflow. Narrow ear canals (Shar-Peis, Bulldogs) trap debris. Hairy ear canals (Poodles, Schnauzers) hold moisture and wax against the skin. If your dog has any of these traits, prevention needs to be more aggressive.

Foreign Bodies

Grass awns, foxtails, and plant material can work their way into the ear canal during walks. A sudden-onset head shaking or pawing at one ear — especially after a hike — should raise suspicion for a foreign body. This almost always requires a vet to remove, as the barbed shape of grass awns means they only travel deeper with movement.

Underlying Medical Conditions

Hypothyroidism, Cushing's disease, and autoimmune disorders can all predispose dogs to chronic ear infections. If your dog's ear infections are bilateral (both ears), recurrent despite good management, and accompanied by other symptoms like hair loss or weight changes, a veterinary workup is warranted.

How to Clean Your Dog's Ears Safely (Step-by-Step)

Cleaning your dog's ears isn't complicated, but technique matters. Done wrong, you can push debris deeper, irritate the ear canal, or rupture the eardrum. Here's the safe way:

  1. Gather your supplies. You'll need a vet-recommended ear cleaning solution (more on that below), cotton balls or gauze pads, and treats. Never use Q-tips, and never pour anything into the ear unless you're sure the eardrum is intact.
  2. Choose the right cleaner. Not all ear cleaners are created equal. Look for a solution that's pH-balanced for dogs, contains drying agents (like boric acid or salicylic acid) for maintenance, and doesn't contain harsh irritants like alcohol or hydrogen peroxide. Vet-recommended ear cleaners on Chewy →
  3. Position your dog. Find a quiet spot. Have your dog sit or lie down. Gently hold the ear flap upright to straighten the L-shaped canal — this gives the solution better access.
  4. Fill the ear canal. Apply the solution generously until it just starts to overflow. Don't let the bottle tip touch the ear — that contaminates the bottle.
  5. Massage the base. Gently massage the base of the ear for 20–30 seconds. You'll hear a squishing sound — that's the solution breaking up wax and debris deep in the horizontal canal. This is exactly what you want.
  6. Let your dog shake. Step back and let your dog shake their head. This helps fling loosened debris out of the deep canal. It will be messy — that's normal.
  7. Wipe, don't dig. Use a cotton ball or gauze pad to gently wipe the inner ear flap and the opening of the canal. Only clean what you can see. Never insert anything into the ear canal.

Give your dog a treat and praise after each ear. Positive association makes future cleanings dramatically easier.

Dog Ear Infection Home Remedies — Safe vs. Avoid

When your dog's ear is red and bothering them, it's tempting to grab something from the kitchen. Don't. Here's what's actually safe and what you need to avoid.

✅ Safe Home Care

  • Warm compress — A clean cloth soaked in warm water, wrung out, and held gently against the outside of the ear can provide mild pain relief for surface inflammation.
  • Omega-3 fatty acid supplements — Fish oil and EPA/DHA supplements support skin barrier health and reduce systemic inflammation, which can help prevent ear infections long-term. Shop omega-3 supplements for dogs →
  • Probiotics — Gut health directly impacts skin health and immune function. A quality canine probiotic may help reduce allergy-related ear infections. Browse dog probiotics →

❌ Home Remedies to Avoid

  • Hydrogen peroxide — Kills healthy tissue and delays healing. It also creates moisture that worsens infections. Just don't.
  • Apple cider vinegar — Too acidic for inflamed skin. On broken tissue, it burns. Off the shelf, it can also introduce more yeast into the ear.
  • Tea tree oil and essential oils — Many are directly toxic to dogs even in small amounts. Tea tree oil poisoning is a real thing in dogs.
  • Q-tips / cotton swabs — Pushes wax and debris deeper into the horizontal canal. Can cause impaction and eardrum rupture. Vets see this complication constantly.
  • Human ear drops — Different pH, different active ingredients, different microbial targets. Human drops can damage a dog's ear or mask symptoms without treating the real problem.
  • Coconut oil — While it has mild antimicrobial properties, it traps moisture and creates a greasy film that makes it harder for the ear to dry out naturally.

When to See a Vet for a Dog Ear Infection

Some ear issues can be managed at home. Some cannot. Here's how to tell the difference. See a vet if:

  • You see yellow or green pus or blood in the ear
  • The ear has a strong foul odor
  • Your dog shows significant pain (whimpering, snapping, refusing to let you touch the ear)
  • There's swelling at the base of the ear or the ear flap feels hot
  • Your dog has a head tilt or is walking in circles
  • Your dog has recurrent ear infections (3+ times per year)
  • Your dog is lethargic, has a fever, or has stopped eating

An uncomplicated ear infection treated early typically costs $150–$300 at the vet (exam + medication). A chronic case requiring cytology, culture, and follow-up visits can run $500–$1,500. If you don't have pet insurance, those recurring visits add up fast. Check our Pet Insurance Estimator → to see what coverage would cost for your dog.

The vet will likely: examine the ear with an otoscope, take a swab for cytology (looking at discharge under a microscope to identify bacteria vs. yeast vs. mites), and prescribe appropriate medication — usually topical ear drops, sometimes oral antibiotics or antifungals for severe cases.

One critical note: Don't clean your dog's ears right before a vet visit. The vet needs to see the discharge as-is to diagnose accurately. A fresh cleaning can make it impossible to tell yeast from bacteria or mites.

How to Prevent Dog Ear Infections

Prevention is where you win. Once you understand your dog's specific risk factors, you can build a routine that keeps ears healthy year-round.

Weekly (5 minutes)

  • Visual check + sniff test — Flip the ear flap and look inside. Healthy ears are pale pink with minimal light brown wax. Take a sniff — you're looking for any change from normal.
  • Dry after water — If your dog swam or got a bath, dry the ears thoroughly with a towel. For water-loving dogs, use a drying ear solution after every swim.

Monthly (10 minutes)

  • Gentle cleaning — Use a maintenance ear cleaner like Virbac Epi-Otic or a gentle wipe. Don't over-clean — once a month is enough for most dogs. Over-cleaning strips protective oils and can cause maceration.
  • Ear hair plucking — For Poodles, Schnauzers, Cocker Spaniels, and Doodles, ask your groomer to pluck ear canal hair regularly. Wax adheres to hair, and the hair prevents it from working out naturally.

Seasonally

  • Allergy season (spring/summer) — Ramp up ear checks. Pollen allergies flare and so do ears. If your dog is on allergy medication, be consistent.
  • Diet review — Consider a limited-ingredient or hypoallergenic diet trial if you're seeing recurrent issues. Our Pet Calorie Calculator helps you adjust portions when switching foods.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a dog ear infection heal on its own?
Rarely. Mild cases (minor redness, no discharge) might resolve as the immune system fights off the overgrowth, but most ear infections will persist or worsen without treatment. The risk of waiting is that a simple outer ear infection progresses to the middle ear, which is much harder and more expensive to treat. If symptoms haven't improved within 24–48 hours of home care, see a vet.
How long does a dog ear infection take to clear up?
With proper treatment, most uncomplicated ear infections improve within 3–7 days. Complete resolution typically takes 10–14 days. Yeast infections (Malassezia) may take longer — sometimes 2–3 weeks. It's critical to complete the full course of medication even if symptoms seem to have resolved, or the infection will bounce back. Always follow up with your vet for a recheck if recommended.
Can I use apple cider vinegar on my dog's ear infection?
No. Apple cider vinegar is too acidic for inflamed ear tissue and will cause significant stinging and burning. It can also disrupt the ear's natural microbiome, making infections worse over time. If you need a safe maintenance cleaner, use a product specifically formulated for dogs like Virbac Epi-Otic or Zymox — they're pH-balanced and much gentler. Shop gentle ear cleaners →
Are dog ear infections contagious to other dogs or humans?
The infections themselves (bacterial or yeast overgrowth) are generally not contagious. However, ear mites are highly contagious between dogs and can also be transmitted to humans in rare cases. If your dog has dark coffee-ground discharge and intense itching, have them checked for mites — and keep them separated from other pets until the diagnosis is confirmed.
What does normal dog ear wax look like?
Healthy dog ear wax is light brown to tan, with a mild, slightly waxy odor. It should be present in small amounts — a thin coating on the visible part of the ear canal. Abnormal signs include: dark brown/black wax, yellow or green discharge, excessive amounts that fill the ear, or any foul or sweet-smelling odor. How much does it cost to treat a dog ear infection at the vet?
An uncomplicated ear infection typically costs $150–300 for the exam and medication. If cytology (ear swab microscopy) is needed, add $30–60. Chronic or severe cases requiring culture and sensitivity testing, deep ear flushing under sedation, or oral medications can run $500–1,500+. This is where pet insurance makes a meaningful difference — the monthly premium ($25–60/month for dogs) is often less than a single emergency ear infection visit. Compare pet insurance costs →
Can I clean my dog's ears with baby wipes?
Not recommended. Baby wipes have the wrong pH for dogs, contain fragrances and preservatives that can irritate, and don't have antimicrobial or drying properties. If you need a convenient wipe option, use pet-specific ear wipes — Earthbath makes good ones with witch hazel and aloe. Shop ear wipes for dogs →
Why does my dog's ear smell like yeast or cheese?
That distinctive musty, sweet, or cheesy smell is almost certainly Malassezia — a yeast overgrowth. It's one of the most common causes of ear infections in dogs and tends to recur in dogs with allergies or floppy ears. Yeast infections require antifungal treatment (not antibiotics, which only target bacteria). A vet can confirm via cytology. Keeping ears dry and using a drying ear cleaner weekly can help prevent yeast from overgrowing.
